OBJECTIVE:

  • Design and build an automated frisbee-launching system as a team of four during the 2022 spring semester at RIT.
  • Understand and apply the D.M.A.D.V. process (Define, Measure, Analyze, Design, Verify).
  • Accurately launch a 5-inch diameter disk to a designated target using an electric-powered mechanism.

MY CONTRIBUTION:

  • Performed all RPM and torque calculations for the launch mechanism.
  • Researched and selected realistic values for friction coefficients and spring constants.
  • Determined the relationships between disk weight, system geometry, and launch requirements.
  • Developed an analytical model to ensure the disk would not slip and would launch correctly.
  • Verified final results and documented calculations in a professional engineering format.

PROCESS:

  • Designed a disk launch mechanism where a motor spins continuously at high RPM to launch the frisbee when triggered.
  • Calculated required RPM using:
    • Assumed launch velocity of 14.1 m/s
    • Radius of the disk: 2.5 inches
    • Converted to RPM using rotational velocity formulas, arriving at 2124 RPM
  • Calculated required torque using:
    • Equation: Torque = 0.5 * Roller_Diameter * (Total_Friction + μ * W * g)
    • Estimated coefficient of static friction (acrylic-rubber): 0.6
    • Calculated three friction values based on material interactions and spring compression force (F = k * x)
    • Total friction calculated to be 11.41
  • Final required torque: 10.54 lb·in

RESULT:

  • Prototype successfully launched disks, but underperformed in distance due to insufficient motor torque.
  • Motor used had 3500 RPM (more than required) but lacked the needed torque, limiting launch to ~1 foot.
  • Provided hands-on experience with real-world design trade-offs and the importance of torque in motion systems.
